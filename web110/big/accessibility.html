Web Accessibility

Imagine shopping online with no pictures. You click on a link that says “graphic” or a long string of numbers for a file name only to be brought seemingly nowhere because where you have gone is not being explained to you in a way you can understand. Or maybe you try having the news read to you only to have sections for world news, US news, and opinion each read as “link”, “link”, and “link”.

According to the National Federation of the Blind, there are around 7 million people with a visual disability in the United States. For those who are visually impaired, and for the total of about 56.7 million Americans with a disability, (roughly 17.5% of the entire US population) there are huge chunks of the internet that just disappear and aren’t accessible at all.

In 1999, the National Federation of the Blind sued AOL, making the case that online accessibility was a civil rights issue. The company eventually settled, edited its software, and hired its first Director of Accessibility. That same year, the World Wide Web Consortium developed its first set of Web Accessibility Guidelines.So just like public buildings are required to have wheelchair ramps, web developers were now expected to add accessibility features to their code.

Jason McKee, chief marketing officer of Accessibility Shield says this about web accessibility, “In its simplest form, a website visitor should be able to unplug their monitor and mouse and navigate your company’s website. If you can’t do that or don’t understand what I mean, then you have an accessibility issue.”

When websites don’t contain good coding, it makes unlabeled images, links, or buttons uninterpretable. So whether someone is using a screen reader, voice recognition, or navigating a site by keyboard only, it’s important that your content be navigable by as many people as possible. Doing so not only shows you care about accessibility, but could potentially increase revenue by attracting new users or customers, or even shield your company from a potential lawsuit.
